Practice vs Frustration

Question: Does more practice reduce frustration, or does frustration decrease simply with time?

Understanding the Visualization

This visualization challenges the common assumption that "time heals all wounds" by examining whether frustration decreases due to the passage of time or due to actual practice. The X-axis shows the number of problems solved per session (a measure of active practice), while the Y-axis displays my self-reported frustration level on a scale of 0-5. The data is sorted chronologically using Array.sort() so that the connecting line shows my journey through time, allowing us to see whether time or practice drives the emotional change.

Additional Visual Encodings: Circle size represents confidence level (0-5 scale), while circle color uses a red-to-green gradient that reinforces the frustration measurement. The chronological connecting line is crucial—it shows that early sessions (represented by points on the left with high frustration) occurred first, while later sessions (points on the right with low frustration) occurred after I had accumulated more practice solving problems.

Key Observations from the Data

Observation 1 - Time Alone Doesn't Reduce Frustration: The most striking pattern appears in the left portion of the chart where several data points show frustration at level 3-5 even though multiple days had passed since I started learning. Sessions on October 17, 20, and 22 all show frustration at level 4-5, despite being spread across an entire week. These points cluster at 2-5 problems solved, indicating that low practice output maintained high frustration regardless of how many days elapsed. If time were the key factor, we would expect frustration to decrease steadily across these dates—but it doesn't.

Observation 2 - The Practice Threshold Effect: A clear pattern emerges when examining the relationship between problems solved and frustration. All sessions where I solved 0-5 problems show frustration ranging from 1-5 (highly variable), but every single session where I solved 8 problems shows frustration at exactly level 1 (consistently low). This suggests a threshold effect: once practice reaches a certain level of productivity (8 problems per session), frustration becomes minimal and stable. The sessions with 0 problems solved (October 24 and 30) show moderate frustration (level 2-3) despite occurring late in my learning timeline, further proving that time alone doesn't guarantee reduced frustration—actual problem-solving practice does.

Conclusions

The visualization definitively answers the research question: practice, not time, is the key to reducing frustration in learning to code. Multiple pieces of evidence support this conclusion. First, frustration remained high (level 4-5) across multiple sessions in the first week despite time passing, because practice output remained low (2-5 problems). Second, frustration dropped to its lowest point (level 1) only when practice reached its highest level (8 problems solved), creating a clear correlation between practice quantity and emotional state. Third, the two concept-learning sessions (0 problems solved) occurring late in the timeline still showed moderate frustration, proving that the mere passage of time doesn't guarantee reduced frustration.
